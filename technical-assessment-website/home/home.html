Disciplines

In fencing, there are three disciplines: foil, epee, and saber. Each has unique qualities that make the style different from the rest.

Foil
Foil is the style that most beginners start with--not because it is easy--but because it emphasizes having good point control and technique. Foil has the smallest target area of the three as you can only score a point by touching your opponent's torso with the tip of the blade. Right of way, which determines which fencer got the point when they both get a touch at the same time, is also important to foil.

Saber
In saber, the whole upper body is target area and you score points by touching your opponent with any part of the blade. Because of this, slashing is the primary way of scoring points, unlike thrusting in foil and epee. Saber also uses right of way, just like foil, for when two fencers get a touch at the same time.

Epee
In epee, the whole body is target area, making it have the most target area of the three weapons. Similarly to foil, only the tip of the blade is used to score points, so there is no slashing. Unlike foil and saber, there is no right of way. This means that if both fencers get a touch at the same time, then they both get a point.

Gear

White Uniform
No matter what style you are fencing, the same white uniform is used. This includes a jacket, knickers, plastron, knee high socks, and a chest protector if you are female.

Weapon Specific Gear
On top of the basic white uniform, each discipline has its own gear it uses. The most important difference is the weapon and the lame.
In foil, the weapon is light-weight, with a button on its tip that allows the fencer to score a point when it is clicked against their opponent's target area. The style also uses a lame, which is a conductive jacket that only covers the target area, which is the torso.
The weapon used in saber is full fully conductive, which allows a fencer to score a point as long as any part of the blade touches their opponent's target area. Saber also uses a lame, which covers the torso and the sleeves. The mask in saber is fully conductive as well, since it is target area.
Similar to foil, the weapon in Epee has a button at the tip which forces the fencer to only score points with the tip of the foil. Since the whole body is alread target area, no lame is worn.

Techniques

There are many different types of techniques used in fencing. A few of the most fundamental are on guard, advancing, retreating, lunging and parrying.

On Guard
On guard is the ready stance in fencing. To start a bout, you must be in on guard position. This involves having your front foot facing toward your opponent while your back foot is shoulder width apart and perpendicular to the front foot. Then, your legs should be slightly bent. This position allows for good balance and all other techniques done it fencing are done from on guard.

Advance and Retreating
Advancing is the forward motion in fencing and retreating is the backward motion. To advance, you first move your front foot and then the back foot follows. This is the same, but opposite for retreating.

Lunging
Lunging allows you to cover a lot of distance and make an attack. To lunge, you first extend your arm toward your opponent. Then, your lift your front foot and push off with your back leg into a lunge position. Finally, you extend your back arm to maintain balance.

Parrying
A parry is a defensive action that blocks the opponents attack. Sometimes, it is followed by a riposte, which is when you parry and then touch the opponent back. In fencing there are eight types of parries, some of which are only used in specific disciplines. One of the most commonly used parries is parry 4.
